Playful playing experience

May 2025 — Live coding is the practice of playing with a programmable software in real-time, often in front of an audience. Whether it is instantly modifying computer graphics, hot-reloading during Web development, listening to music while it is being composed, or recompiling a module in production on the Erlang virtual machine, live coding properties are a great way to improve the crafting process and make using a computer system more enjoyable.

I believe that a software should offer both batch processing and reactive live coding, as the reactive part mainly is a loop which calls the batch processing at certain intervals if possible. This is great for testing and scripting too.

My own, your own, their own

May 2025 — When thinking about levels in the programming culture, I concluded by saying that it is a matter of defining the primitives we want to build our system upon, and trying to implement them in the targetted platform's programming system. Yet, this is a practice that is rarely done, as most people instead depends on external primitives that are out of their control, which ultimately leads to complexity in the long run.

Such an issue is mainly caused by failing to recognise and understand our dependencies, especially the most important ones: the operating system and its drivers, the programming language and its runtime, and the translation system (compiler or interpreter). These define a lot of primitives which we should be careful of using everywhere. Instead, we should limit our dependencies on them by only relying on those to implement our high-level primitives!

Our system (implemented in the high-level primitives) must stay in control, calling the low-level implementations as dependencies, not the inverse. And to do so, the high-level primitives should be imperative not declarative. I should be able to say where, what, and how to draw on the screen. The low-level system would just give me the screen. This idea also reduces the total amount of code existing on all layers of the stack: I implement what I need to draw while the underlying platform provides me the simplest memory buffer possible without having to create complex and generic algorithms to fit the needs of everyone (which is currently what is happening with CSS and the likes).

Additionally, this simplifies the preservation of the system in the long run too, as targetting a new platform is simply a matter of me implementing my own high-level primitives using the new platform's features, instead of having to recreate all the algorithms on this new platform and then fixing all the declarative issues and bug since all platforms aren't perfect. Think PostScript, not HTML.

And some may think that it is a hard task, but you could use some third-party library that does it for you, as long as you can control it (everything in the user-space, no OS drivers). The same goes for favouring a declarative paradigm: nothing stops you from building a declarative macro system atop your imperative primitives, as Lispers have been doing for ages. As long as you retain control on the stack.

Denotation of the problem

May 2025 — Languages are important in our day-to-day lives since they allow us to express and share our thoughts. Kenneth E. Iverson even won a Turing award talking about it. In his paper, he presents five criterias to judge a notation: how easy it is to express the constructs arising from a problem, how much suggestive it is, how much importance does it give to irrelevant details, is it economic, and whether or not it helps with writing formal proofs.

While I do not believe in all of the five points, I think notation is a major part of our languages that lacks research. We often limit the discussions around a language having prefix, infix, or postfix notation, but never do we go further: how much adaptations should a programmer have to do in order to interact with a computer?

And this is the abstract place where the language semantics are relevant, especially whether or not you will reformulate the problem to map well with the language, or try to shape the language to solve the problem.

The fight for simplicity

May 2025 — Rarely do you not hear about simplicity. Everyone is claiming their solution to be simple, yet it's another Rube Goldberg machine. So here's my participation in trying to understand the concept of simplicity: simplicity is an organic solution given a constrained environment.

Simplicity is not about beauty but being useful: the elegance of a system relies in its simplicity, not the inverse. Simplicity is not about size: a reliable air traffic controller is not a web server despite both being programs. Simplicity is not inherited: complexity is natural, simplicity isn't, you must confront it. Simplicity is not about naivety: you are naive until you know better. Don't solve a problem you don't have yet, but when you have it, don't go around it.

But if simplicity is hard to achieve, it is still a goal to strive for as it offers easier maintainability, evolution, onboarding, and flexibility when the specification isn't complete. It's a long fight, but a fair one.

You interact with a compiler in order to update a database

May 2025 — Algorithms + Data Structures = Programs; or at least this is Niklaus Wirth's assertion, one he strongly believes in up to being the eponym of his book. And I definitely agree with him, not that I would argue against him in the first place.

In fact, I'll go further and say that a system is an interface to represent and act upon records of data. Think of the interface as a compiler (algorithms), and the records as a database (data structures). Mathematicians may even think in terms of categories and functors. Therefore, when designing a system, you have to decide about the notation it offers and the data which defines it.

Keeping everything in one big monolithic repository

May 2025 — I like to have everything related to a project available in the same place, for offline use. This includes the code with the dependencies vendored, the tools and scripts, the documentation, the bugs and reports, the artistic assets, as well as the build artifacts. It gives me flexibility and uniformity during the creation and maintenance process.

I know however that this is not a commonly agreed upon decision, mainly due to the use of inapt systems, such as Git for centralised programming. I feel like people should explore other tools to find their needs, because Git is such a bad tool for most use-cases in general, and yet everyone requires it...

That's why if I can get away with it, I simply store everything in a directory on my local computer, sometimes I do backups and archives on external drives and cloud storages. If I can't, then I try to use Ark VCS or Perforce Helix, the former being my favorite.

But really, consider keeping everything in one big directory first, and using better suited tools than Git for your centralised engineering, it is criminally underappreciated. Not that I dislike Git itself.

Levelled programming

May 2025 — The computer programming world is too often segregated into a higher and a lower level depending on the distance between the programmer and the hardware. People even try to define programming languages using these levels. Wikipedia has even invented a concept of programming language generations?!

I find these definitions to be nonsensical and disconnected by giving to much attention to irrelevant details: are machine codes high-level because most processors have been micro-coded since the '60s? Instead, I'd like to present a new definition centered around systems and its users.

On one hand, I believe high-level programming refers to playing in the abstract world of the system, from its user interface to its database. It is the place where we define a set of abstraction primitives and builds upon it. On the other hand, low-level is the driver code, the glue code, the part of the system which is invisible yet is the most important. It is the implementation of the defined primitive abtractions on the targetted platforms.

Therefore, the goal is to find the right set of abstractions we want to use (no more, no less), and implement it, while also constructing the system. Be wary to not make your higher levels depend too much on the lower level, instead prefer the inverse, to keep control over your entire system.

Compositional user interfaces

May 2025 — One thing that I particularly enjoy is the composability of a user interface's actions. Something similar to Vim verbs, or PostScript concatenation notation. I find a certain joy in it, and others does too given the existence of code golfing.

Composition is not primitive key-bindings, it's about having a language and writing sentences with it. You have a central data-structure and hundred of words to act upon it. Academics often talk about expressing the data flow.

And this is well represented with tools like Vim (motions operating on the text objects), the UNIX shell and its pipes (chaining commands operating on raw textual streams), Forth systems (highly-refactored and minimal words operating on two stacks), and APL (transparent polymorphism and terse infix notation to express mathematical expressions with joy). UNIX hackers shall remember Master Foo's quote: "There is more Unix-nature in one line of shell script than there is in ten thousand lines of C."

Don't get fooled by the examples being mostly textual, this composition can also be found in more visual and abstract mediums too: mixing colours in order to get a new one, smashing waveforms together to make music, or sending messages to objects à la Smalltalk! It's just a matter of creativity after all...

{Us,modd,hack}ers

May 2025 — I typically classify the people interacting with a system in three groups in order to better understand their needs and expectations. I do not consider any group to be better than another one, especially since we often go from one group to another.

Firstly, there are the users, the ones acting upon the system in order to achieve their goal without too much consideration. They benefit from smart defaults and fast interfaces.

Then comes the modders, the creatives making the system do anything they want from its primitives only. They compose and mix stuff altogether to create a dish they'll love. And as we all know, a dish is better when it doesn't come with an aggressive licensing model :)

And finally its the hackers, the people directly delving into the system's architecture and low-level aspects. They don't simply combine primitives, they create the primitives.
